TITLE: Marginal Product of Labor
BODY:
  - 17
  - Data From the Tesla Production Function:
NOTES:
    In the short term, Capital is fixed. The only way to scale up production is to hire more labor, and when you do so with a fixed amount of capital, you bump up against the law of diminishing marginal returns. Each person will have fewer and fewer machines to work with, so the product of each additional worker will be lower. That does not mean the total return will decline, unless the marginal product of labor (MPL) becomes negative.

--- Slide 18 | Layout: Title and Content ---
TITLE: Diminishing Marginal Product of Labor
BODY:
  - We hold one input fixed (capital) and…
  - Use more and more of a variable input (labor)
  - Then total output will increase by less and less
  - i.e., the Marginal Product of Labor (MPL) goes down
  - Note: MPL is the slope (d𝑄/d𝐿) of the output curve

--- Slide 72 | Layout: Title and Content ---
TITLE: Economies of Scale
BODY:
  - What happens to long-run average costs (LAC) as output increases?
  - Economies of scale: LAC falls with output
  - Constant economies of scale: LAC is the same for different output levels
  - Diseconomies of scale: LAC rises with output
  - Economies of scale may result from:
  - Falling input prices as output grows
  - Technological reasons (« Returns to scale »)
  - 72
NOTES:
    Economies of scale refer to the impact of a higher rate of output on long run average costs.
    cost elasticity wrt to output > 1 if diseconomies of scale
    cost elasticity wrt to output < 1 if economies of scale
    So economies of scale is a cost-elasticity concept. Increasing returns to scale are a technological concept – relating quantity of output to quantity of inputs. Increasing returns to scale are a factor in bringing about economies of scale, but it’s not the only factor. TITLE: Economies of scope
BODY:
  - Produce two or more products  Avg. cost goes down
  - Ability of a firm to produce a portfolio of related products cheaper than if each product were produced by separate, independent firms
  - Benefit from sharing capabilities across products:
  - Input production
  - Engineering know-how
  - R&D
  - Marketing
  - Example: Airbus A380 and A318
